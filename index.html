Dear Reader,

Here are my concerns:

That if I ask you to forget, the first thing you will do is remember. The first thing will call up the second, the third and the fourth and a timeline will be established.

Linearities sneak up on you; they organise, order and arrange without you noticing ; allowing things to be easy to follow, making you expect simplicity. They frustrate me and I will try to avoid them.

I am looking for ways to express my thoughts like a walk, I am looking for ways to tell a story about time, where the I stands for the personal pronoun used by a speaker to refer to himself or herself but it is not limited to the 9th letter of the latin alphabet, the subject or the object of self-consciousness ; the ego, a line, the electric current or iodine. From perspectives that don’t assume humanity and avoid anthropomorphising.

Think of baroque polyphonic choirs, think of entanglements.

My voice being one of them, not the principal. As the author of this text, this is the only time I will exercise my authority over you, to offer you the following reminder. Partially because I am concerned with how much of a good job I have done and partially because I don’t know how accountable you will hold me. I would advise for very, and urge you to look closely through each line, for when I fail to do as I preach.

Now saying this, here is where we stand. You should forget and proceed to read with no assumptions and prior knowledge of how things are ought to be or you should keep on remembering that you know and you assume.

Welcoming remarks,
Victoria

the foreground

Since its establishment as a scientific concept by Newton, time has gained its objectiveness through being uniform, infinite, continuous and effortlessly divisible1. Time gains some of its other characteristics from Christianity, its foundation in the now is predominately influenced by the Christian vision of the world where earthly time and eternity, are separated, since ‘eternity cannot be measured in segments of time, it is an attribute of God’2 disconnecting time from past and future.


The mechanised clock itself was built in order to gain preciseness in praying.

In 1339 Heinrich Suso wrote a manuscript about a clock (“Horologium Sapentiae / Wisdom’s Watch upon the Hour”) that serves as a means of communication between man and the divine order. The clock translates the divine order into language that the ordinary man can understand. Particularly when it is time to pray3

The same quest for accuracy and preciseness that created the clock for punctual praying. Has moved us from the imprecise mechanical clock to the extremely precise, atomic clock, recently building one that will probably outlast our Sun. Having spend the last 680 years building the most precise clock possible, to capture time, we have advanced from approximate errors of 15 min per day to 1 second per 10 billion years4.


Time seems to exist independently of us, it is universal; an hour is an hour, is sixty minutes, three thousand six hundred seconds indifferently to whether it was spend productively or dreadfully5. You would imagine that as our time is astrological and literally reflects the rotation of the Earth that it would be grounded in our understanding in such way but, it isn’t, time seems to exists anywhere else but here.


Contemporary man is more than capable of dealing with time recklessly at ease. We seem to have mastered time, created it, figured out how to measure, divided it with great precision, and trace it back million of years to its creation and yet somehow we seem to lack the understanding of its temporality or how is can be perceived differently.


side thought

June 30th 2012 was the third ‘leap second’ incident after almost a decade of not having any. As the Earth’s rotation around itself actually takes 24 hours and 2 seconds instead of just 24, every now and then the International Telecommunications Union (UTI) has to add a second to even things out 6.


This is necessary due to the existence of two times: Greenwich Mean Time, which alines noon with the time when the sun is at it’s highest position in Greenwich, London and was the time all official clocks followed until the of fall 1967. And Coordinated Universal Time (UTC), which was adapted by several countries after 1976, and is calculated as 1 second of mean solar time at 0° longitude 7.


Due to the fact that the later clock takes into account the Earth’s rotation a difference is created between the two clocks: less than a second in a year, up to 30 seconds since the implementation of UTC turns into minutes and hours as the year and millennia go by 8.


It was that leap second 23:59:60 that caused Reddit to be in different states of off for about 2 hours. Reddit, the popular discussion website runs on the operating system Linux. Originally the glitch that drove the servers into hyperactivity was attributed to something else, but after a while the true cause was found. In one of the most basic levels of the operating system a linux kernel, the hrtimer, could not understand what the extra second was.9.

hrtimer is a subsystem that used when an application is “sleeping,” waiting for the OS to complete some other task. In some cases, it sets a kind of alarm clock for these sleeping applications that will go off when the OS is taking too much time with its other work.”10

What seems to have happened is that when the leap second arrived, the hrtimer became a second ahead of the OS, started awaking several sleeping applications, running the system into hyperactivity. The solution came by eventually restarting the servers11.


The problem with leap seconds is that programmers built the core kernels which have to deal with them in the 1970s, when leap seconds weren’t a thing yet. They were built with the expectation that time always moves forward, time cannot go backwards and time cannot repeat itself : all principles that the leap second renounces.12


Mozzilla, The Gawker, Stumble Upon, Yelp, Foursquare and Linkedln, all also carried confused hrtimer kernels that caused similar problems12. Google hoping to avoid major problems has created an antidote called the “leap smear”. It operates by modifying Google’s NTP servers, specifically the Network Time Protocol which

periodically checks the computers’ time against a more accurate server, which may be connected to an external source of time, such as an atomic clock13

and adds occasionally a couple milliseconds making so that when the leap second finally arrives Google’s clock will have already accounted for it14. It is a 20 hour linear smear, 72,000 SI seconds that eventually add up to the required leap15.


In a sense the leap smear slows down the hrtimer to meet with with the Earth’s rotation. Making Google’s servers, clocks and computers for 20 hours at a time completely in tune with the landscape they exist upon more than the humans who programmed them.

If it wasn’t for software we would have very little or any at all interaction with leap seconds, or by extend the rotation of the Earth for that matter. Our electronic devises and clocks update automatically. Even if they didn’t, nobody would be able to notice that they are running a second ahead. This is mostly due to the fact that humans inhabit social time instead of astrological time16.

[S]ocial time express the change or movement of social phenomena in terms of other social phenomena taken as points of reference17

Social time is manifested in expressions like ‘let’s meet during lunch’ or ‘i haven’t seen him since highschool’ or ‘right after the Obama administration’ all indicating points of time in respect to social events rather than astrological time. Lone astrological time references like 30/06/2012 or 1982 or March 2nd become significant only when they are put within social references18.


Different groups, depending on location, social structure, religion etc. have different social references.

a metropolis demands a frame of temporal references entirely different from that of a small village19

Big general concepts that effect everyone, become staples of social time in urban settings such as work hours, weekends or holidays. Smaller events like natural phenomena, birthdays or which fruits are in season fail to reach the common social time system.


Social time is not ‘uniform and homogenous’ like astrological time. It is heterogenous, allowing several different systems and kinds of time to exist parallel and interwoven to each other. Greek immigrants talking to each other on the train can remark upon not seeing their families since Clean Monday (27/2/2017) while looking forward to seeing them again at Koningsdag,(27/04/2017) and understand each other as they are part of two social time understandings.


23:59:60 as a SI second falls under astronomical time but as ‘leap second’ as an event it enters the social time sphere, creating a moment of common reference in our western social time not centred around humans, that is as rare as a leap second.


Our perception of time — social and astrological — solemnly follows the rhythms of human social life. 9 at work is 9 at work, irrelevant to whether it is dark or light, or papayas are in season.

progress is a forward march , drawing other kinds of time into its rhythms. Without that driving beat we might notice other temporal patterns […] agnostic about where we are going, we might look for what has been ignored because it never fit the timeline of progress20.

Perhaps it is the driving beat of progress or the authority of the clock that doesn’t allow us to experience temporal patterns within our rigid timelines.


Yet within the heterogeneity of social time immigrants and indigenous people have succeeded in avoiding that singular uniform homogenous beat.


Sámi have inhabited those temporal patters for years. Partially due to marginalisation and discrimination and partially due to their occupation, reindeer heading that does not allow to tune out from other kinds of time and only focus on the driving beat of human progress.

Progress is embedded too, in widely accepted assumptions about what it means to be human. […] we learn over and over again that human are different from the rest of the living world because we look forward - while other species, which live day by day, are thus dependent on us21

Their social time follows mutually the cycles of human and non-human environments. Weekends are not social time staples because during the rounding season or the earmarking, rest days, if any, are uncertain.


Preciseness or punctuality is traded in for variability, proximity and temporality. Mazzullo and Inglod observe that to represent a few moments, Sámi would use an expression meaning ‘the lighting of a pipe’ (pii’po-tsâhk ’ke ’tâm-pod’dâ) and a longer period would be ‘the time needed to brew coffee’ (käf ’ fevuos’ sâm-pod’dâ) and depending to where those expressions were used, it would range from a few minutes (village) to a period of few hours (forest)22

.

Time is interwoven into activities, it is culturally understood that tasks or travelling might take longer than anticipated. When a Sámi travels through the boreal forest, he or she can provide only vague indications on how much time it will take to arrive and to return, the vastness of the forest region is such that a traveller cannot anticipate if something will occur or when23

.

Duration is another notion, that is treated differently within the Sámi understanding of time preciseness is so embedded in the western thought of time, that tasks and activities tend to demand a finite start and end unlike the reindeer herding, which as a nature of work cannot be dictated by a clock marking the start and the end.

[W]hen Sámi people go to the forest from reindeer separation or earmarking of calves, no-one would ever look at their watches. [..] In winter, the reindeer separation would be started in pitch darkness and it would still be dark when the work was finished’24

It would be easy here to argue that the Sámi understanding of time is circular and it evokes a sense of local infinity that western understanding doesn’t, to create images of Sámi living without clocks and beyond time, fundamentally opposing the West. To follow an argument like that would play right into the hands of the dualistic discourse in which time has always been discussed in.

[T]he logic of that argument being that there exists a clear-cut and structured division between western and non-western understanding of the world, running from the very categorical distinction between modern and traditional societies, to the more subtle difference between a supposedly western understanding of time as linear, cumulative and irreversible and the non-western notion of time as cyclical, non-cumulative and reversible25

yet the world doesn’t work like this, even though the West likes to write about it in that way.


The Sámi have spent years in contact or as part of the western world. Suffered from marginalisation and forced assimilation, years of timely issued taxes and fines to be payed in order to be allowed to live in their land. Legislation that did not allow them to speak their language or practise their religion. Nowadays Sámi have jobs outside reindeer herding, they serve as teachers or doctors or lawyers. They have jobs that require them to follow at 9 to 5 schedule.


The Sámi are very much aware of western time systems, and their unwillingness to follow them is not be romanticised as a “truer” or “simpler”. It functions within the heterogeneous social time.


The Sámi perception of time, much like Sámi culture, is founded in the landscape they inhabit. Time does not appear as a mystical powerful force that determines everything. As seen in Valkeapää words ‘time does not exist ’, not because time is elsewhere or time is elusive, but because time is ‘located’. The centre lyric of the poem with the single word ‘land’ carries much of the weight of time and location: time is experienced like the landscape, it is as familiar as the forest, the present as a form of duration blends in with the past and the future and it is representation in the flora and the fauna of the boreal forest, the past never ceases to exist as it is found in the repetition of activities conducted in the forest and the forest itself. Time is located ‘here’.


When time is located here, it involves ‘living day by day’ and being entangled with concepts of indeterminacy and precarity. One is open to the possibilities of encounters, that encourage adapting and transforming. That result in actions or occupations have an undetermined beginning and end, and still are, like going to work everyday, repetitive.

[B]ut they are also open-ended responding to the opportunity and encounter […] such indeterminacy expands the concept of human life, showing is how are transformed by the encounter26

living so closely with the land calls for a precarious living

[P]recarity means not being able to plan. But is also stimulates noticing as one works with what is available27

When life in enveloped in indeterminacy and precarity, the focus is shifted from being precise or punctual to being alter and responsive, being able to notice and act accordingly quickly are valuable social skills.


Sámi traditional knowledge in terms of snow and reindeer herding reflects practicing such living :

So when somebody says that there is cuoŋu (“strong- crusted snow”) now, then the other person immediately knows that the snow is now so hard that it provides an excellent surface to move and travel on, but at the same time he or she also knows that in such conditions the reindeer move quickly over great distances, which, in turn means that the herd must be tightly managed to avoid it running off or breaking up and scattering. These terms contain references to the interaction between reindeer, man and land.28

The myriad of Sámi terms like cuoŋu, (See Sámi Snow Terminology) used to describe precise snow characteristic are crucial in respect to grazing, mobility and tracking. Identifying quickly such factors is important as snow conditions directly affect the survival and well being of the reindeer. The traditional Sámi snow terms identify snow characteristics in regard of food access, mobility, tracking, visibility, season and snow layers29.

“Čearga can be so hard that neither reindeer nor people can dig through the snow. In areas with čearga, reindeer are “locked out” from grazing on the plants beneath the snow. This causes poor feeding conditions for the reindeer. The thickness of a čearga layer can be from 5 cm to one meter deep. Čearga conditions affect the mobility of both reindeer and humans as it is easy to travel on this type of snow. Consequently the herders have to be very careful not to lose individual reindeer to neighbouring herds. However, in terms of tracking capabilities, čearga is unfavorable. It is difficult to see where individual reindeer have been walking. Čearga is at the top of the snowpack, but may include as much as half the snowpack when grazing conditions are poor. The term čearga is used in winter, from January to April.” 30

As captured by the snow terminology, within Sámi culture, preciseness is attributed in terms of spatial characteristics (weather, snow, conditions, herd location etc) and not in terms of time (09/07/2017 or 6 o’clock)31. Making it so that if there is čearga perhaps a trip will be postponed until it passes or a herder will choose to keep moving in search of another terrain instead of resting.


In the few studies conducted about the Sámi understanding of time (see Ingold & Mazzulo, Mazzulo, Bergaman), the Sámi are credited with their own perception of time, phrased in a spectrum of different levels of romanticisation. All researches seem to agree if you are not talking about time the same way as the West is, you must then have a different perception of time. That type of thinking paints of a picture of social time as uniform and homogenous while perhaps unwilling propagates exclusion and marginalisation of minority groups and indigenous peoples.


The Sámi perception of time does not differ from the western perception of time, what creates a difference between the two, is the point of references of each social time. The fact that non-humans and spatial characteristics hold the same importance as human centric events within Sámi time, makes it so that it would be impossible to impose ‘preciseness’ as it is understood in the western world, ‘marked by exactness and accuracy of expression or detail’.


It seems hard to grasp, that if time is not precisely quantitive, can be of any good use. Yet it is not time that generates preciseness but the clock and especially, the tyranny it extorts through its connection to social and political control32. You have to be at work everyday at 9 in order to assist the forward march of progress, and that is only possible if it is precise and accurately measurable. Sámi time is a qualitative local system, that does not need to be scalable or homogenous. Sámi time should be explored as a part of the multidimensional social time, and not as existing outside of it.


Social time, since its introduction as a concept, has mainly been explored in terms of duration, direction, form and shape. Sorokin and Merton point out that :

“Most social scientists have proceeded on the tacit assumption that no system of time other than those of astronomy or imperfectly related calendar is possible or, if possible, useful. They have assumed a time, the parts of which are comparable, which is quantitive and possessed of no qualitative aspects, which is continuous and permits of no lacunae. […] [I]n the field of social dynamics such restriction to a single conception of time involves several fundamental shortcomings” 33

Attributing a different perception of time to the Sámi, because their signifiers and attitude towards time are different is such a shortcoming. Social time as a concept is wide enough to include local systems, temporal patterns, non-human and spatial characteristics point of references, and if not it is important to stretch the western scientific idiom to include them.

the end

This work has looked a lot into polyphonies as a methodology of overlaying several different voices in telling one song or more accurately in this case one story.


As Anna L. Tsing, who brought my attention to polyphonies, observes :


“To appreciate polyphonies one must listen both to the separate melody lines and their coming together in unexpected moments of harmony or dissonance” 1

Tsing brings up polyphonies early on her incredibly fascinating book The Mushroom at the End of the World: On the Possibility of Life in Capitalist Ruins when discussing assemblages and how

““patterns of unintentional coordination develop in assemblages, much like in musical polyphonies”2

since that early mention of polyphonies, I have been captivated by their possible of being used as a methodology as not just as a metaphor for complex systems.


Polyphonies as opposed to monophonies, consists of two or more simultaneous indented melodies, where it is hard to trace which melody is the dominant of the the principal one. Musically polyphonies were used a lot in the Middle Ages and the Renaissance as a musical texture.

“harmonically interdependent, yet independent in rhythm and contour.”3

Polyphonic signing also referred to an ancient, archaic or old style singing that follows the methodology of polyphonic arrangement is widely used with the Balkan folklore music. Slightly more complex in style, it involves several different voices in different ranges and tones along side with

“antiphonies, call and responses, drones and parallel intervals.”4

Allowing the songs to be layered with multiple storylines that are not bound to linearity.


Usually when one traces the history of polyphonies, one would stop with polyphonic signing neglecting to get into account hip hop that often follows the practice of polyphonic arrangements. Kanye West as a producer is often credited as the best in layering multiple voices, at time even voices and are not entirely human. It seems important to point the distinction between, West as a rapper and West and a producer. As a rapper he is one of the voices in the composition, equal with everyone and everything else in the song. As a producer he takes a step back, which allows him to view the song as a whole and compose.


What polyphonies can produce so beautifully and Kanye West does so masterfully is to capture something intriguingly.

Ultralight Beam, widely talked about as perhaps Kanye’s best song, is a combination of everything West had explored until that point. It involves a choir, one that West has assembled himself this time.”5

What West gains from assembling his own choir is character and drama, in places beyond where we are used to look for character and drama; the principal character or voice. The choir does not serve as a filler in the background. It shifts the vocal landscape giving you the impression that once they are close to you and the next moment away, situating the voices in space. Ultralight Beam follows voices through their stories. Rather than limiting our attention to one voice or the relationship of two it forces the listeners to focus on the assemblage, on the gathering of voices.

“Kanye layers voices and he doesn’t limit them to singing or rapping, they become the base, the percussion or anything required in the song.” 6

It moves on by rejecting principality, you would expect that when the song reaches its peak moment, West as the artist’s who the song is credited to and owner of album in which it will be included, would take the lead and rap, yet he doesn’t, instead he gives the space to Chance the Rapper. West also avoids to sign off the song, the epilogue is provided by Kirk Franklin, with the very last lyric sang by the choir. Within the 05:20 minutes of the track, West covers only 01:24 of the whole song.


What I see here besides interesting producing, is a methodology to write about the world as complex system. A series of tools that would allow us to explore systems and share their stories, from a multi - perspective inclusive of humans, non-humans, technological systems, ideas and so one and so forth. I am not interested in transporting the entire body go musical theory and composition into the roam of writing, I am interested in the possibility of using polyphonies to construct dynamic stories.


Of course, I am not the first one to ever think so. Fiction writers have been utilising polyphonies to structure narratives for years. Miklail Bakhtn introduced the concept of the polyphonic novel back in 1934, as a reading of Fyodor Dostoevsky’s work.

“polyphony is a feature of narrative, which includes a diversity of points of views and voices” 7

The method have been utilised in several different ways by several different authors, J.M Coetzee, Milan Kundera, Paul Williams, Etgar Keret, are just some of them. Recently Belarusian author Sventlana Alexievich was awarded the Nobel Price in literature “for her polyphonic writings, a monument to suffering and courage in our time”


What is common within the work of these writers is their tendency to “allusive” and “repetitive”, choosing to expand forms and notions over a more “direct” or “logical” or “economical” presentation.


It seems almost comical to me how we expect narratives to be “economical”, how the scientific idiom, or perhaps its aura — not completely sure how much economics qualifies as science — has leaked in all aspects of human life, including fiction writing. The understanding of “economical” as ‘using no more than necessary’, reflects the scientific goal of always seeking the simplest solution, Occam’s razor8. Newton, who has earlier mentioned for establishing astronomical time, pretty much established Occam’s razor, as a scientific principal in his work Pricilla Mathematica and polluted all kinds thinking with this logic.


This quest for simple and elegant solutions, assumes that there is a direct connection between simplicity and truth9. What it mainly does through the elimination of complexity and excess is to remove site specific content. And make things widely scalable and applicable to all. To expect fictional stories to be shaped in such way, seems almost unbelievable to me


What seems necessary is to find a way to dismantle the scientific idiom in a manner that would allow complexity and lack-of-scalability to exist and to be considered valuable10.


Eira’s wonderful doctoral thesis, The Silent Language of Snow : Sámi traditional knowledge of snow in the times of climate change does so by being written in both Sámi and English. In that way advancing Sámi as a language in which science is conducted. An important step for indigenous peoples and minorities towards moving into the scientific arena and meeting science in their own terms11. Further Eira demonstrates how Sámi traditional snow terminology includes advances and complex snow science terms, in a way defining Occam’s razor. And how it is important for the national adaptive strategies for climate change to incorporate different ways of knowing, including both traditional knowledge and scientific knowledge12.


Tsing observes how science and scientific studies are conducted so differently in East and West, that western scientists avoid Japanese studies even though they are the leading experts in field matsuke mushrooms by offering the following instance :

“when criticising[,] American scientists comment about Japanese studies […] ‘are too descriptive’. Descriptive means site-specific, that is, attuned to indeterminate encounters and thus non-scalable” 13

I feel in a way, polyphonic writing is an antidote to Occam’s razor and the monophony of the scientific idiom. It creates the form for busy and complex answers, that require the reader to disassemble them. Occam’s razor and other “economical” solutions assist simplicity and straightforwardness to an extent that I at times find frustrating. That type of writing praises simple and scalable explanations, forbids tangents from the subject or explorations of something that is partially fitting, but not aiding the progress the story forward.


This work has tried to follow all this tools — failed or succeeded at times — by implementing the idea of polyphonic composing to writing. By including quotes as raw samples, crediting the sources of thoughts directly and not paraphrasing them. Citing references even when a text or an author has provided inspiration for form or format and not content.


By trying to disrupt the traditional structure of an argumentative essay, creating way to unpack notions and ideas, that are usually treated in segments. Allocating time to build me own “choir”, inclusive of humans, non-humans, landscapes, technological comportants, poetry and music. To build a complex story of complicated and elaborated characters.


To tell a story of three times, that of the landscape, of Sámi time and of the West, as if one could see them unfold as one walks by. Focusing on patterns and systems that don’t always follow the desired simplicity and predictability of the west. In this slow matter of developing it has worked through time scales that are unwitnessed by humans, from the pattern of forest developments to SI seconds. Offering a peek into otherness.


This work aims to create an understanding of the heterogeneity of time. And how time like everything else can be understood from several different perspectives. To serve as a remedy for the type of thinking that demands simplification in order to understand.


This walk starts in media-res, so let’s say, you walk down a path and you run into a snag, a dead tree, a unexpected drawback — a soarvi, begins with us already in conversation, yet it doesn’t matter what we were discussing before diving in, before running into the soarvi, the big or peculiar standing dead tree, that will remain a point of references in our walk. As we try to find our way around or through the fact that you know. The fact that your knowledge, knowledges all your future knowledge14.


Our soarvi, standing in the middle of our walk, between the foreground, the end, the picture and the background.


Always starting at the middle and finishing before you start, provides the opportunity for you to be aware of form and just content. To understand perhaps how things are before knowing what they are. And for me, to invite you to practice the art of noticing, a form of awareness and sensitivity towards details, and I explore in all the bodies of my work.


To interrupt you with interludes and side thoughts to reenforce that linearities and easy to follow sequences should not be expected. To learn all things from the beginning, out of an alphabet book, letter by letter, concept by concept, thought by thought.


To zoom out and to understand that science sometimes paints a bigger picture leaving out all the participating characters and drama, and that if you look closely, humans might not even come into the picture till the very end.


To be aware of an elaborate background that is busy and active and not there to server as a backdrop to a single hero’s triumph journey.


To understand the language and if not to be aware that you don’t


To realise the context and the interest that motivated telling this story comes from personal and subjective experiences.


To be alert how site-specific this story is, and at the same time that is spanned over three different communities of Sámi, all distinctive from each other, trying not to summarise them all under an all-inclusive term.


To draw a circle large enough, where “idiom” could be ‘a group of words established by usage as having a meaning not deducible from those of the individual words’ and ‘the dialect of a people or part of a country’ and ‘a characteristic mode of expression in music or art’.

s new snow. Vahca causes bad travel conditions, but makes it easy to see where the reindeer have gone (track). Vahca can soften hard snow. The reindeer feel free to walk in different directions under such conditions.


Vahca

is new snow. Vahca causes bad travel conditions, but makes it easy to see where the reindeer have gone (track). Vahca can soften hard snow. The reindeer feel free to walk in different directions under such conditions.


Čearga

is created by strong winds which have blown the snow and rolled it to become hard. Čearga is so hard that neither reindeer nor herders can dig through it. Where čearga occurs, grazing is prevented and reindeer are “locked out” of the pasture. Čearga can have a thickness of one meter.


Seaŋáš

occurs in the winter, when it has been cold for a while, then the seaŋáš process (seaŋkut) starts. Refrigeration promotes this process. In spring, the wind can change the snow to seaŋáš. The snow is grainier and is not stuck to the ground. It is easy to move it and the reindeer can easily dig through seaŋáš to access food.


Sievlla

is very wet snow which occurs when the weather has been mild and the snow has begun to thaw completely through. The snow is wet and it is hard to walk on because a person or animal will sink completely through the snow to the ground.


Soavli

is a mix of snow and water in which the snow melts from the bottom of the snow upwards. It is important to be careful when traveling in soavli, because the snow can be deep on the upper side of soavli, but soft and wet at the very base.


Bihci

(ground surface frost) occurs when the ground is snow-free and cold. The cold conditions form bihci on the surface of the ground. The use of the concept Bihci is entirely associated with conditions on the ground


Ritni

(frost on trees) is primarily formed on trees from warm moist air interacting with very cold snow surfaces. Specific weather conditions lead to the formation of ritni. Ritni sometimes occurs when there are is bare ground, but the snow must have been deposited before the warmer air arrives. If it has been cold for a long period of time, then “ritni” wood will occur and then it becomes difficult to see anything at all.


Njáhcu

is wet snow which occurs as a result of alternating snows and thaws. This prevents reindeer from grazing. Should there be njáhcu in occurring in connection with All Saints Day, it will become a bad winter because subsequent rain and warmer, then colder temperatures will form geardni (ice) and produce bodneskárta conditions.


Geardni

is a thin ice layer on the top surface of the snowpack. It occurs when it rains on the snowpack and then the rain freezes. The snow surface first becomes wet then frozen resulting in a thin ice layer on the top of the snow.


Gaskageardani

is a condition in which one or more hard ice layers within the snow pack which occurs when it snows on top of a geardni ice layer. If the ice layer in the middle is not softened, but is still hard, it will limit the reindeers „ability to dig through the ice to access their forage. There may be several layers within the snowpack.


Bodneskárta

is a condition that occurs in the fall when the early snow first melts across the ground and plants, then that water freezes, forming a hard coating of ice. The reindeer are not easily able to break through this layer to reach their food and this leads to a rudneguohtun winter.


Moarri

is a condition in which a crust is formed on the surface of the snow as a result of alternative thawing and refreezing of the top layers. The crust is not quite hard enough to support the weight of people or reindeer and will cut the feet when one breaks through. The reindeer avoid going into moarri and will wait until it becomes cuoŋu. If you are trying to move the herd, it becomes very difficult to do so because it is hard for the reindeer to move through this type of snowpack. Moarri is almost hard enough to support the weight of the reindeer, but breaks once they step onto it.


Skáva

is the formation of an ice layer or snow crust from sun melt at the surface of the snowpack during the day, followed quickly by cooling later in the evening. Skáva conditions are usually followed by cuoŋu conditions within the same day.


Cuoŋu

snow conditions are different forms of melting snow which develop when the weather is very warm, sunny, and mild then followed by rain. The snow then becomes sievlla (and on the lakes it becomes soavli). But subsequently, when it becomes very cold and that snow freezes, it will be almost like ice. Then, it is good traveling conditions for the reindeer. But difficult traveling conditions for the herders. In the forest areas, cuoŋu develops first under these conditions..


Oppas

Two types of oppas (untouched snow) are: 1) One type of oppas describes an area where there has not been re-feeding. The snow is soft and it is easy for the reindeer dig through the snow pack to reach the food at the bottom of the snowpack. These conditions enable the herd to move in pastures for feeding. 2) Another type of oppas is characterized by very deep snow which makes it difficult to travel because it is necessary to wade.


Seakŋut

is a verb which describes the process by which Seaŋáš changes the snow so that it becomes more porous, and both bodneskárta and geardni disappear.


čiegar

is hard snow which has been heavily trampled by reindeer and, thus, compressed into snow so hard that the reindeer are not able to dig through it to access the plants and the herders should avoid these areas. Sometimes the čiegar conditions can change to a more favorable snow state for grazing, and if it is an area rich in lichens, then the reindeer can be allowed to graze there. In the very rare cases where an area with small amounts of snow develops into čiegar conditions and the lichens are abundant, it is possible to let the reindeer graze this area before Christmas, but not throughout the winter. Reindeer cannot be returned repeatedly to these thinner snow areas to graze because the bottom of this snowpack will be frozen too hard to permit access to food.


Bearta

is an intermediate condition which involves both bare ground and/or snowpack, which occurs in the fall after it has been snowing but when the weather has become milder again. The snow and ground become like ice - rough and hard. In some areas, it is bare ground; and in others, snow. It is difficult to see tracks and it is difficult to travel. The reindeer tend to walk a lot when it is bearta.

the bigger picture

Situated south of the Arctic, the boreal forest region forms a continuous belt across the North America and Eurasia, amounting to 11% of Earth’s terrestrial surface and one of its largest biomes. Generally the arctic, subarctic and or mid-latitude regions with a cold climate are referred to as the boreal forest and are found in Russia, Canada, United States, Finland, Sweden, Norway and Iceland (Burton et al. 2010). The circumboreal forest accounts for about one quarter of the world’s forested area, with a size of almost 1 billion hectares (Burton et al. 2003 as cited in Bergeron & Fenton 2012).

The establishment of the boreal forest region dates back to the mid-Pliocene when the decline of the Cenozoic temperature allowed the warmth-demanding trees of the Tertiary era to be replaced by the genera that is typically present nowadays in the boreal forest region (Crawford 2008).

Forests then largely disappeared from the high latitudes throughout the Pleistocene, placing all the forested area of the time much souther, however research on pollen records has shown that probably minor shrubs and herbs permitted the survival of pockets of tree in higher latitudes during the Last Glacial Maximum (Crawford 2008).

The succeeding Holocene enabled trees to resettle the north but never as far as Pliocene. However, during the early Pliocene, the tree migration was vast and rapid across both Europe and Canada allowing the boreal forest to advance far into the tundra biome. Now given that the cold weather has prevailed for the past 6000 years the boreal forest hadn’t had the opportunity since to forge ahead, until very recently as a side effect of the rise of temperature due to global warming. However that doesn’t mean the boreal forest had remained static all over those years. (Crawford 2008)

The boreal forest and the arctic tundra, are both major biomes which in total cover 30 million square kilometres in the northern hemisphere; fundamentally different in flora, fauna and ecosystem productivity levels, have kept mostly apart, due to different thermal characteristics needs (Shugart et al. 1992), however as the climate warms the possibilities for the boreal of rest to advance into ‘the zone between the northern limit of the boreal forest (taiga) and the southern extent of the arctic tundra [..] (that) stretches for 13 400 kilometres around the northern hemisphere and across three continents’ (Crawford 2008) rises.

the picture

First the icebergs retreated, gigantic masses of frozen water pulled up towards the north leaving behind bare mineral land.

Then the trees started spreading, quietly and peacefully, seed by seed, wind-blow by wind-blow, the seeds landed in over thousands of acres and 3 continents.

There was no overnight occupation, no grand skim of enslaving a land, just some wind, and some luck for a seed to land in land and not on a melting iceberg. There was also a mushroom, turning rock into nutrition, that without the seed wouldn’t have blossomed, that was pretty crucial.

And then came soil, mostly frozen for 10 months of the year, but the remaining two of 24 hours of sun were enough for the seed to turn into an infant, and for the infant to turn into infants. And so in the far north and circumpolar regions a forest grew out of pine seeds that carried mushrooms.

And as the forest are and blossomed, the pines worked the soil rich, and the forest bedding thick with smaller plants and bushes, now spruce also grew. And then fauna came, and started clearing the bedding floor, but once fauna was around it was figured that careful selection of small plants brings in the type of fauna you want.

You did so, first by small fires, but that made sense, it had happened before, occasionally, and it helped the forest grow. Then there were more fires, here and there and parts of the forest were transformed.

Deciduous now they were, shedding their leaves all winter, pilling them up on the forest floor. During the rainy summer no burning was possible, but during the dry summers the hills were covered in smoke.

And so the pine decreased, and the burned spruce made room for birch, clear fields allowed for grazing and in glades now grey alder and juniper grew.

But the wind blew and those areas were surrounded by a birch forests and those forests by pines and those pines by spruces that were now climbing all the way up where the icebergs still stood.

By the time the fauna had grown custom to being around, and the forest bed was cleared. Fences were needed to round-up the reindeer twice a year.

And by the time the fences were made, the tree trunks were called wood, but just for seldom use.

But before the wind blew again, it was called timber.

Now that the pine was timber, the wind didn’t blew often enough, and the forest was to be grown for other purposes such as efficiency and extensiveness besides just chance and luck (Björn 2000).

The forest was now called underproductive and the swamps a waste, because timber didn’t grow there fast enough.

But there was space, and the felling tall pine was good, and making more of it just made sense, simplification and uniformity were things that the forest didn’t have enough, and was to be given (Björn 2000).

And “forest was divorced from nature, nature from its cycle, from the annual and daily rotation” (Björn 2000)

Soon, spruce was good enough and smaller trees and shorter trunks, mainly because there weren’t enough virgin forest, since the furthest and furthest and more remote untouched ones were now for a while begging called logging sites. There, where the pine had grown thick, and the wind still blew at its own pace, now were roads and squares and lamp posts.

And by 1970, all sites were good enough and the forests and the swamps and the mires, all the trunks were salvageable (Björn 2000).

And “forests belong to biology and ecology” (Björn 2000) and were to be regulated, because we were starting to run out them. So we categorised and named them, to keep track of them.

And so natural preserves were declared, and parks, and industrial forests and experimental forests and pseudo-traditional landscapes of virgin forest. And now the forest is again plenty and we can all have some, and tell our friends that ‘the forest cover in Finland is more extensive than in any other European country. Three fourths of the land area, some 23 million hectares, is under forests’ (Björn 2000).

And this is how things look like now. And this is how forests are now.

the background

Somewhere south of the Reisa National Park, near the the border with Finland and the Norwegian county Troms is the source of the Alta River. Know as Alta River; Álttáeatnu; Alattionjoki, Alattionjoki, Guovdageaineatnu or Eatnu along its 250 km run to Alta, to flow into the Altafjorden and by extend the Barents Sea. The river starts in the Kautokeino mountains and runs by the villages Kautokeino and Masi, from the high Finnmarks plateau to the Sautso Canyon, that begins just downstream from the Alta Power Station.

The Alta Power Station is perhaps one of the most controversial hydropower developments in Norway’s history, as it is even stated in the company’s website. When it construction was proposed in 1970 by the Norwegian Water Resources and Energy Directorate, it was just “the latest affront in a long history of Norway’s marginalisation of its indigenous peoples” (see Sámi State Conflicts)(Lawrence 2011)

For the Sámi, the construction of Alta Dam meant the displacement of their village, Masi, and serious impacts on their way of life. Their agricultural and fishing industries would be lost, as would traditional reindeer herding areas and the seasonal migration routes used by herders.

As a journalist covering the protests against the dam, Niillas Somby understood that those were the things this grandfather used to talk about, when he said : “you have to have respect for the river, for the land” (Somby 2014).

In his talk at the Documenta 14 panel 34 Exercises of Freedom : # 3 , Niillas Somby traces his life growing up as a Sámi and how the long discussions with his grandfather, taught him what is important to the traditional Sàmi life. He explains : “the main thing in being part in the traditional reindeer herding family is to have respect, very very very deep respect to the land, to the stones, to everything living and non-living thing in the nature”(Somby 2014)

Although the Sámi activists succeeded in scaling down the project, and saving Masi by 1976, the battle against the Norwegian Government and the construction of the dam continued till the autumn of 1982 and ended when the Norwegian Super Court decided that Sámi had no legal claim to their pastoral land (see Alta Controversy Timeline). That was 6 years of active mobilisation after their homes had been saved from induction. Why would a nation subject themselves to that ? To answer that one has to understand what ‘home’ is for the Sámi.

When Somby speaks of home in the beginning of this talk, he speaks of lávvu, a conical temporary tent-like dwelling that until the late 1930s was the only type of housing the Sámi used. Currently the majority of Sámi and Somby himself most probably live in a permanent four wall house, yet lávvu have not disappeared, they are still used actively by reindeer herders and fishermen.

As soon as the reindeer men had put up the lávvu, a place (báiki) would be formed, and a fire would be lit and coffee quickly brewed […] Yet while the lávvu, brought to life by its inhabitants, gather theirs activities into a focus of dwelling, the forest in which it stands is no less homely. Thus a Sámi person does not leave home to go to the forest.[…] For Sámi, home is open-ended and unbounded. (Mazzullo & Ingold 2008)

As the lávvu is erected in the forest in the midst of working it centres its inhabitants, reindeer herding is an around the clock occupation that provides few scarce moments of rest, the lávvu is the type of precarious dwelling that allows anyone to rest when the chance is given. A herder doesn’t have to go home, like an office clerk goes home to rest. The notion of home is not directly connected with the nation of a precise permanent dwelling nor with the nation of ownership. There seems to be no need to separate “my land” from “not my land”, even now that Sámi inhabit permanent dwellings there are barely any fences.

Which might be explained by the fact that

from an indigenous perspective the landscape is not an intimate backdrop or substrate against of which animate being perform their respective tasks” (Mazzullo & Ingold 2008)

Observations that you may find echoing in Niillas Somby statements : “People and animals are as much part of the landscape as are rocks and tress” (Somby 2014). For Sámi, who understand their land complexly, there was no difference between inducting their homes and destroying the pastoral lands or lakes; the forest is as much their home as is their dwellings. Their forests take their names from the activities that are conducted in them, from ‘herding forests’ to ‘fishing forests’ to even treeless forests like the open tundra downstream of Masi (Mazzullo & Ingold 2008). As their life and culture in inseparable from the forest, to effect the forest means to effect the Sámi.

Meaning that the power plant would effect them even if their “roofs over their heads” were not effected. Something that the Norwegian Government, the Norwegian Court System and the Norwegian Water Resources and Energy Directorate seemed not to understand, that the environmental consequences of the hydro plant would directly effect the Sámi culture (Paine 1982), perhaps because from the beginning that cultural or social considerations were not high on their priorities(Briggs 2000).

Sámi culture is centred around reindeer herding. Being the first animal to inhabit the land after the ice retreated, the Sámi have created a close relationship with the animal as it was their main source of clothing and food. Reindeer herding is all year around labor intensive operation that demands understanding of complex herd and natural behaviours. Having spend decades interacting and observing herds, the Sámi traditional knowledge of behavioural patterns extends so deeply that herders are able to predict herd movements. Patterns that span over time and space in the annual cycle of herd migration that require the herders to be in tune with their surrounding and to react to them quickly (Paine 1982).

As Robert Paine observes in the report he was asked to write about the possible consequences of the Alta dam :

Carefully laid plans are brought to nought by bad weather, by the intrusion of another herd — or by any one of a number of disturbances in the terrain that are increasingly common these days ( people, dogs, vehicles, machines)

These patterns are exactly what the dam effected, the crucial reindeer migration routes, that are in themselves an intricate combination of pasture, weather, herds, space and time. Each terrain chosen carefully for different reasons ranging from adequate feeding and resting to giving birth and refraining from mixing with other herds while accommodating both the needs of the herd and the herders (Paine 1982).

So there laid a crucial difference between what the government saw as a space scarcely populated, as a location, in the north that was forecasted to need more electricity in the future and a solution, a hydro plant which was argued to be the most efficient and inexpensive way to do so. And what the landscape actually was for the people who inhabited it, an active participant in the Sámi life system.

Norwegian’s government narrow vision, exercised by conducting no prior research before the dam’s construction, made it possible to only focus on the desired aspects of it. Governments have a long tradition of abstracting and simplifying their inhabitants — human and non human— in the name of being eligible and by extent manageable (Scott 2008). Making the Sámi life, where humans and non-humans are complexly entangled in terms of space and time completely intangible.

In the few assessments about the implication of construction of the dam prior to its development, construction roads, electrical cables and transportation vehicles all crucial parts of the construction and operation of the dam where not included as aspects to be assessed for potential damage (Briggs 2006). Which reinforces the idea that the NVE and the Government addressed the project as it would be excluded and separate from the landscape and it was set to be built in.

To understand the impact of a detail like roads, in such a grand skim one has to think of roads not as just passive components but as an active participants to the system they are embedded in. Building roads creates accessibility. It disturbs the reindeer migration along side with other species that are crucial to sustaining the local environment and Sámi families, such as lichens & mosses, berries, fish and birds. It brings along the greater public and the network of state cabins.

What the government failed to understand is that Sámi culture is interwoven in reindeer herding, and reindeer herding is a system that involves humans, reindeer and land as active participants. And land encompasses at once the concepts of landscape, nature, space and terrain. When dealing with a culture in which casual descriptions to the landscape are so precise and elaborate that they can serve as maps (Gatski 2004). It is almost naive to try to treat its people with any less complexity.

For the native dweller, the landscape tells — or rather is — a story. It enfolds the lives and times of predecessors who, over the generations, have moved around in it and played their part in its formation. To perceive the landscape is therefore to carry out an act of remembrance, and remembering is not so much a matter of calling up an internal image, stored in the mind, as of engaging perceptually with an environment that is itself pregnant with the past (Ingold 2010).

and like all stories, it takes time.
